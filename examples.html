2. Sample Texts

Here are some attempts at translating famous English literary excerpts into Čeluria. Due to limited vocabulary and grammar, they often include partial English or forced usage.

2.1 Poem: Robert Frost, “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening” (1923)

2.1.1 English Original

He will not see me stopping here
To watch his woods fill up with snow.
My little horse must think it queer
To stop without a farmhouse near
Between the woods and frozen lake
The darkest evening of the year.
I give my harness bells a shake
To ask if there is some mistake.
The only other sound’s the sweep
Of easy wind and downy flake.
The woods are lovely, dark and deep,
But I have promises to keep,
And miles to go before I sleep,
And miles to go before I sleep.

2.1.2 Čeluria Translation

Ra x miru ma zai ču nara,                  
ta meke-lu nara “nubi.”
Ma de ñoka x tera lifa-lu?
x ču x mive, 
nara šivo, bile šivo,
bile darkest zaly.
ma daje ligu me,
shen x tera re?
solu kale meke-li,
tofi nubi sudi-lu?
nara meke, šivo, raku,
ma keep fory,
zaly dame chu ma x šeka,
zaly dame chu ma x šeka.

2.1.3 IPA Transcription

/ʁa x ˈmi.ʁu ma za.j tʃu ˈna.ra/
/ta ˈme.ke.ly ˈna.ra nu.bi/
/ma de ˈɲo.ka x ˈte.ʁa ˈli.fa.ly?/
/x tʃu x ˈmi.ve,/
/ˈna.ra ˈʃi.vo, ˈbi.le ˈʃi.vo,/
/ˈbi.le ˈdaʁ.kest ˈza.ly/ (English “darkest” forced in usage)
/ma ˈda.je ˈli.gu me,/
/ʃen x ˈte.ʁa ʁe?/
/ˈso.lu ˈka.le ˈme.ke.li,/
/ˈto.fi ˈnu.bi ˈsu.di.ly?/
/ˈna.ra ˈme.ke, ˈʃi.vo, ˈʁa.ku,/
/ma kiːp ˈfo.ʁy,/
/ˈza.ly ˈda.me tʃu ma x ˈʃe.ka,/
/ˈza.ly ˈda.me tʃu ma x ˈʃe.ka/

2.1.4 Notes

  • Using “nubi” = “milk” for “snow.”
  • “darkest” partially borrowed from English, etc.
  • Lines referencing “miles to go” → “zaly dame chu” (“many roads go”).

2.2 Prose: Jane Austen, Pride and Prejudice

2.2.1 English Original

It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife. However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighborhood, this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered as the rightful property of someone or other of their daughters.

2.2.2 Čeluria Translation

Ma xifa sense, ona ra de gari me fira duma. Chang ta x tera ra de rika, dyro ma me soka-lu be? Truth me rika vane, zra x tera ra me neme-lu? Ra kehu de someone de femu me, ta ma x shili-lu?

/ʁa ˈke.hu de/
/ˈsʌ.mwan de ˈfe.mu me,/

  • Used partial English words like “truth” to fill gaps.

2.4 Prose: Lewis Carroll, Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland

2.4.1 English Original

Alice was beginning to get very tired of sitting by her sister on the bank, and of having nothing to do: once or twice she had peeped into the book her sister was reading, but it had no pictures or conversations in it. “And what is the use of a book,” thought Alice, “without pictures or conversation?” So she was considering, in her own mind, whether the pleasure of making a daisy-chain would be worth the trouble of getting up and picking the daisies, when suddenly a White Rabbit with pink eyes ran close by her.

2.5 Prose: Charles Dickens, A Tale of Two Cities

2.5.1 English Original

It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair, we had everything before us, we had nothing before us.

2.5.2 Čeluria Translation

Bile me šyro-lu, bile me xalo-lu, bile me shyo-lu, bile me nyno-lu, bile me rebi-lu, bile me xifa-lu, bile me šivo-lu, bile me xibo-lu, bile me sipa-lu šyro, bile me nufe-lu xalo, zma guo gemu-lu, zma x gemu-lu.

2.5.3 IPA Transcription

/ˈbi.le me ˈʃy.ʁo.ly, ˈbi.le me ˈxa.lo.ly,/
/ˈbi.le me ˈʃjo.ly, ˈbi.le me ˈɲy.no.ly,/
/ˈbi.le me ˈʁe.bi.ly, ˈbi.le me ˈxi.fa.ly,/
/ˈbi.le me ˈʃi.vo.ly, ˈbi.le me ˈxi.bo.ly,/
/ˈbi.le me ˈsi.pa.ly ˈʃy.ʁo, ˈbi.le me ˈnu.fe.ly ˈxa.lo,/
/zma guo ˈge.mu.ly, zma x ˈge.mu.ly./

2.5.4 Notes

  • Each “it was the X of Y” → “bile me X-lu, bile me X-lu,” substituting “bile”= “day/time,” “me”= “(link?)”, “X-lu”= “X-like.”
  • Used partial English words like “Light” and “Darkness” for missing vocabulary.
